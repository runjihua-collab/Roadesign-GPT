# Automate Road Design with AI Tools

## 1. Introduction

Road design is a complex engineering activity that requires the coordination of technical data, design constraints, calculations, standards, and professional judgment across multiple stages of work. Even at the level of early planning and alignment selection, engineers must evaluate competing spatial, environmental, economic, and operational factors through iterative comparison of alternatives (Agrawal et al., 2025; Kang et al., 2012). Workflow efficiency therefore matters not only for productivity, but also for the quality, consistency, and traceability of design reasoning. When tasks are fragmented across disconnected tools or stages, the links between inputs, calculations, decisions, and final explanations become harder to maintain.

Recent literature suggests that AI tools are increasingly relevant to both engineering practice and assignment-based tasks because they can assist with information handling, pattern recognition, option evaluation, iterative refinement, and, in some contexts, technical content generation (Daareyni et al., 2025; Naser et al., 2025). Evidence from road design shows the value of AI in specific applications such as optimisation and decision support, while broader civil engineering research points to its potential to assist planning, workflow coordination, and more integrated engineering processes (El-Abbasy, 2025; Hasan & Lu, 2025). At the same time, this literature emphasizes that AI use in engineering must be examined critically because explainability, workflow integration, standards compliance, data quality, and accountability remain significant constraints.

Against this background, the purpose of this literature review is to examine how AI tools may support assignment workflow in a road design context and how such support relates to the longer-term goal of automating road design from basic input information. The review focuses on four linked themes: AI in engineering workflow, AI as a means of connecting knowledge, calculations, design reasoning, and explanation, current applications in road design and related civil engineering tasks, and the expectations, limitations, and research gaps that shape future development. In doing so, the review treats road design not only as a technical design problem, but also as a workflow and knowledge-integration problem in which AI may offer growing, but still incomplete, value.

### Guiding research questions

1. How can AI coding tools support engineering assignment workflow?
2. How can AI tools connect engineering knowledge to calculations, design decisions, and written explanations?
3. How has AI already been applied in road design or related transport/civil design tasks?
4. What are the current expectations, opportunities, and limitations of AI in road design?

## 2. AI tools for assignment workflow

Although the sources used in this review do not focus directly on student assignment practice, they nonetheless suggest that AI-assisted tools can improve workflow efficiency in engineering tasks by integrating information handling, option evaluation, and iterative refinement. In the road design literature, AI supports workflows that would otherwise require extensive manual comparison of alternatives. Agrawal et al. (2025) describe road alignment studies in which data preparation, classification, multi-criteria assessment, and route selection are connected within a broader analytical process, while Kang et al. (2012) show that optimisation tools can reduce time and improve consistency when evaluating competing highway alignments. Together, these studies indicate that AI can help structure complex engineering work, support planning across multiple decision stages, and improve efficiency in tasks that require comparison of constraints, alternatives, and outcomes.

The implications for assignment workflow beyond design evaluation remain inferential rather than directly demonstrated. If AI can organise heterogeneous inputs, process constraints, and help generate technically plausible alternatives, then similar tools may also assist engineering students in planning tasks, organising source material, and structuring analytical work before writing begins. In this sense, the workflow value of AI is not limited to calculation or optimisation; it may also lie in reducing fragmentation across stages of an assignment by helping users move from problem definition to evidence gathering and then to decision-oriented outputs.

Daareyni et al. (2025) strengthen this broader inference by showing that large language model-based systems can translate multimodal inputs into technical design outputs and refine them through feedback loops. Although this evidence comes from CAD and manufacturing rather than academic assignments, it suggests how AI-assisted tools could support drafting and revision in engineering contexts more generally. Tools that can interpret partial inputs, generate structured outputs, and revise them iteratively may help students prepare outlines, refine technical explanations, and improve the coherence of written submissions. However, the current source base does not provide direct evidence on literature searching, citation management, or assignment drafting in road design education specifically. The literature therefore supports a cautious conclusion: AI appears capable of improving workflow efficiency and coordination across stages of engineering work, but its application to assignment planning, literature search, drafting, and revision remains only partly established.

## 3. AI for connecting knowledge, calculations, design, and explanation

The literature suggests that one of the most significant opportunities for AI in engineering lies in its ability to connect tasks that are often separated in practice, including information processing, calculation support, design evaluation, and technical communication. In the road design literature, this integrative role is most visible in optimisation-based workflows. Agrawal et al. (2025) show that contemporary route alignment research increasingly combines data preparation, classification, multi-criteria analysis, and least-cost path modelling within a broader decision-support sequence rather than treating each step independently. Likewise, Kang et al. (2012) demonstrate that highway alignment optimisation can integrate multiple cost and constraint factors in a single evaluative framework, allowing design alternatives to be generated and assessed more efficiently. These studies provide strong evidence that AI-related methods can connect engineering knowledge and calculations to design decisions in road design, particularly where large datasets, competing constraints, and iterative comparison are involved.

However, the same road-focused literature provides only limited evidence that AI currently connects these technical processes to explicit written explanation or design justification. The optimisation models support decision-making by structuring inputs, evaluating alternatives, and identifying preferable solutions, but they do not show in detail how AI can interpret standards, explain assumptions in natural language, or produce a coherent written rationale for why a selected design is appropriate. As a result, the literature supports a stronger claim about AI assisting analytical and evaluative reasoning in road design than about AI producing complete engineering explanations in that domain.

Broader engineering research offers a useful analogy for this missing link. Daareyni et al. (2025) show that large language model-based systems can connect user requirements, multimodal inputs, model generation, and iterative refinement within a CAD workflow. Although this is not direct road design evidence, it suggests a plausible pathway by which AI could connect engineering knowledge, computational steps, design outputs, and explanatory feedback within a more unified process. In this sense, the broader engineering literature extends the road design findings: road-focused studies show that AI can already support knowledge-rich calculation and design selection, while wider engineering studies indicate that newer generative AI systems may also help translate requirements into outputs and support the explanatory layer that traditional optimisation systems do not address well. Even so, the literature makes clear that transparency, precision, and verification remain essential, because stronger integration across knowledge, calculations, design, and explanation also increases the need for traceable and reliable outputs.

## 4. AI applications in road design and related civil engineering tasks

Current literature shows that AI has already been applied most clearly in road design through route and alignment optimisation. In this area, AI-related methods are used to process spatial, environmental, economic, and engineering constraints in order to generate and compare feasible alignment alternatives. Agrawal et al. (2025) show that this body of work increasingly combines GIS, deep learning, multi-criteria decision-making, and least-cost path analysis, suggesting that road design research is moving beyond single-factor optimisation toward more integrated decision-support workflows. Similarly, Kang et al. (2012) demonstrate that intelligent optimisation models can produce highway alignments comparable to conventional manual solutions while reducing time and cost, indicating that AI has practical value in early-stage planning and alignment selection. These studies relate directly to road design because they address a core design task: selecting and evaluating route alternatives under multiple constraints.

At the same time, the literature suggests that current AI use in road design remains concentrated in partial automation rather than complete design generation. The reviewed road-focused studies are strongest in alignment optimisation, data processing, and alternative evaluation, but they provide less evidence for automated handling of broader road design tasks such as detailed geometric design, drainage design, safety documentation, standards interpretation, or integrated written justification. This indicates that AI in road design is presently more mature as a decision-support and optimisation tool than as a fully autonomous design system.

Broader engineering studies help clarify what more advanced automation might look like, even when they are not directly about roads. Daareyni et al. (2025), for example, show that large language model-based systems can translate text and visual inputs into CAD outputs and refine them through iterative feedback loops. Although this is not direct evidence of AI road design, it provides a useful analogy for the longer-term goal of converting basic project information into design outputs through a connected workflow. Overall, the literature shows that AI applications in road design are currently strongest in optimisation and decision support, while broader engineering research points toward the possibility of more integrated design assistance.

## 5. Expectations, opportunities, and limitations

The literature suggests that expectations for AI in road design should be ambitious but realistic. Direct road-design evidence indicates that AI already offers clear opportunities in tasks such as route alignment optimisation, alternative evaluation, and the handling of multiple spatial and economic constraints (Agrawal et al., 2025; Kang et al., 2012). These applications show that AI can improve workflow efficiency by reducing manual comparison effort, supporting more rapid iteration, and helping engineers structure complex decisions more consistently. Broader civil engineering literature extends this opportunity further by suggesting that generative and predictive AI may assist planning, scheduling, technical support, and other iterative engineering tasks across connected workflows (Naser et al., 2025; El-Abbasy, 2025). Taken together, the literature supports the view that AI can enhance productivity, expand access to knowledge, and strengthen decision support, but that current benefits are most credible in bounded tasks rather than in complete autonomous design.

The literature is equally consistent in showing that significant limitations remain. One of the most important is explainability. Hasan and Lu (2025) argue that in civil engineering, predictive performance alone is insufficient because safety-critical decisions require transparency, interpretability, and traceable reasoning. This point is especially relevant to road design, where engineers must justify design choices against standards, safety expectations, and stakeholder scrutiny. The road-design studies reviewed here show that AI can support optimisation and decision-making, but they do not show that AI can reliably explain those decisions in a form suitable for professional justification. Explainability therefore remains a central limitation, particularly where AI outputs may influence design judgments with legal, technical, or public safety implications.

Workflow integration is another major constraint. Although AI methods can perform well in isolated tasks, broader review studies indicate that engineering adoption is hindered by weak integration with existing tools, fragmented processes, and the absence of systematic evaluation frameworks (Naser et al., 2025; Salah & Alabdullatief, 2025). This wider evidence is not specific to road design, but it is highly relevant because road projects typically depend on coordinated use of standards, spatial data, calculations, design software, and documentation. Similarly, dataset limitations continue to restrict reliable implementation. Across civil engineering, researchers identify domain-specific data scarcity, heterogeneous input formats, and insufficient benchmark datasets as recurring obstacles (Naser et al., 2025; El-Abbasy, 2025). These issues matter directly to road design, where reliable AI support would depend on high-quality terrain, traffic, environmental, and standards-related data.

The literature also points to standards or codal compliance and professional accountability as critical barriers to broader adoption. Naser et al. (2025) note the lack of codal provisions for generative AI use in engineering, while Hasan and Lu (2025) emphasize that end users remain accountable for AI-informed decisions. Even if AI can generate alternatives or accelerate workflow, engineers still bear responsibility for checking whether outputs comply with relevant design rules and whether the reasoning behind those outputs is defensible. The most realistic expectation, therefore, is not that AI will soon replace road designers, but that it will function increasingly as an assistive and decision-support technology whose value depends on human oversight, verification, and context-sensitive judgment.

## 6. Research gap

The current literature does not yet address AI-supported road design as a fully integrated process. Existing road-focused studies provide direct evidence that AI can support specific tasks, particularly route and highway alignment optimisation, multi-criteria analysis, and the comparison of alternatives under complex constraints (Agrawal et al., 2025; Kang et al., 2012). However, this body of work remains concentrated on bounded analytical problems rather than on end-to-end design support. It offers relatively limited treatment of how AI might move from initial project information through knowledge organisation, calculations, design reasoning, standards interpretation, and written explanation in one connected workflow.

This gap becomes clearer when the road-design literature is compared with broader civil engineering and construction research. Review studies indicate that AI applications across the built environment remain fragmented, with many models developed for separate predictive, optimisation, or management tasks rather than for integrated engineering workflows (El-Abbasy, 2025; Salah & Alabdullatief, 2025). Similarly, generative AI research in civil engineering points to emerging potential for planning, scheduling, and iterative support, but also identifies unresolved problems in workflow integration, evaluation, codal alignment, and deployment readiness (Naser et al., 2025). These wider studies suggest that the research challenge is not simply to create more AI tools, but to connect them meaningfully across the sequence of engineering work.

Another underdeveloped area is the combination of technical reasoning with explainable outputs. The road-design studies currently recorded in this review show that AI can aid decision support and optimisation, but they do not strongly demonstrate how AI can justify design choices in transparent language, trace assumptions, or produce explanations suitable for engineering accountability. Broader literature on explainable AI makes clear that such capabilities are essential in civil engineering because professional users remain responsible for interpreting and defending AI-informed decisions (Hasan & Lu, 2025). Likewise, broader engineering analogies such as AI-assisted CAD workflows show that it may be possible to convert basic inputs into technical outputs through iterative model generation and feedback, but these analogies do not yet amount to strong road-design-specific evidence (Daareyni et al., 2025).

Accordingly, the main research gap is the lack of strong literature on AI systems that combine workflow support, engineering calculations, design reasoning, and explainable outputs from basic input information within a road design context. Current research is strongest in isolated task support and weakest in integrated, traceable, standards-aware automation. This project is positioned within that gap by exploring how AI tools might support assignment workflow in a road design setting while also contributing to the longer-term goal of transforming basic project inputs into coherent and explainable road design outputs.

## 7. Conclusion

The literature reviewed here indicates that AI already has meaningful value in engineering and road design, particularly as a tool for structuring complex workflows, supporting optimisation, improving the comparison of alternatives, and assisting iterative technical tasks. The strongest road-design evidence concerns applications such as route and highway alignment optimisation, where AI can process multiple constraints and improve decision-support efficiency (Agrawal et al., 2025; Kang et al., 2012). Broader civil engineering and engineering design literature extends this picture by suggesting that AI may also contribute to planning, workflow coordination, model generation, and knowledge-intensive support across connected stages of work (Daareyni et al., 2025; Naser et al., 2025; El-Abbasy, 2025).

At the same time, the overall literature supports AI more strongly as an assistive and decision-support technology than as a fully autonomous road design system. Across the reviewed sources, the most persuasive evidence relates to partial automation, analytical support, and workflow augmentation, while evidence for integrated, end-to-end, standards-aware road design from basic inputs remains limited. This limitation is reinforced by recurring concerns about explainability, workflow integration, dataset readiness, codal compliance, and professional accountability (Hasan & Lu, 2025; Naser et al., 2025; Salah & Alabdullatief, 2025). The current state of knowledge therefore suggests that AI can already enhance assignment workflow and selected road design tasks, but that substantial further research is needed before it can be relied upon as a comprehensive design system.

Future work should therefore focus on developing AI-supported road design approaches that are not only technically capable, but also integrated, reliable, explainable, and standards-aware. In particular, the literature points to the need for stronger research on systems that can connect workflow support, engineering calculations, design reasoning, and transparent outputs within a single road design process. Advancing in this direction would move AI in road engineering from isolated task assistance toward more coherent and trustworthy design automation.
