## 3. AI for connecting knowledge, calculations, design, and explanation

The literature suggests that one of the most significant opportunities for AI in engineering is its ability to connect tasks that are often separated in practice, including information processing, calculation support, design evaluation, and technical communication. In the road design literature, this connection is most visible in optimisation-based workflows. Agrawal et al. (2025) show that contemporary route alignment research increasingly combines data preparation, classification, multi-criteria analysis, and least-cost path modelling within a broader decision-support sequence rather than treating each step independently. Likewise, Kang et al. (2012) demonstrate that highway alignment optimisation can integrate multiple cost and constraint factors in a single evaluative framework, allowing design alternatives to be generated and assessed more efficiently. These studies provide direct evidence that AI-related methods can connect engineering knowledge and calculations to design decisions in road design, particularly where large datasets, competing constraints, and iterative comparison are involved.

However, the same road-focused literature provides only limited direct evidence that AI currently connects these technical processes to explicit written explanation or design justification. The optimisation models support decision-making by structuring inputs, evaluating alternatives, and identifying preferable solutions, but they do not fully demonstrate how AI can interpret standards, explain assumptions in natural language, or produce a coherent written rationale for why a selected design is appropriate. As a result, the literature currently supports a strong claim about AI assisting analytical and evaluative reasoning in road design, but a weaker claim about AI producing complete engineering explanations in that same domain.

Broader engineering research offers a useful analogy for this missing link. Daareyni et al. (2025) show that large language model-based systems can connect user requirements, multimodal inputs, model generation, and iterative refinement within a CAD workflow. Although this is not direct road design evidence, it suggests a plausible pathway by which AI could connect engineering knowledge, computational steps, design outputs, and explanatory feedback within a more unified process. In that sense, the broader engineering literature extends the road design findings: road-focused studies show that AI can already support knowledge-rich calculation and design selection, while wider engineering studies indicate that newer generative AI systems may also help translate requirements into outputs and support the explanatory layer that traditional optimisation systems do not address well. Even so, the literature also makes clear that transparency, precision, and verification remain essential, because stronger integration across knowledge, calculations, design, and explanation increases the need for traceable and reliable outputs.

## 4. AI applications in road design and related civil engineering tasks

Current literature shows that AI has already been applied most clearly in road design through route and alignment optimisation. In this area, AI-related methods are used to process spatial, environmental, economic, and engineering constraints in order to generate and compare feasible alignment alternatives. Agrawal et al. (2025) show that this body of work increasingly combines GIS, deep learning, multi-criteria decision-making, and least-cost path analysis, suggesting that road design research is moving beyond single-factor optimisation toward more integrated decision-support workflows. Similarly, Kang et al. (2012) demonstrate that intelligent optimisation models can produce highway alignments comparable to conventional manual solutions while reducing time and cost, indicating that AI has practical value in early-stage planning and alignment selection. These studies relate directly to road design because they address a core design task: selecting and evaluating route alternatives under multiple constraints.

At the same time, the literature suggests that current AI use in road design remains concentrated in partial automation rather than complete design generation. The reviewed road-focused studies are strongest in alignment optimisation, data processing, and alternative evaluation, but they provide less evidence for automated handling of broader road design tasks such as detailed geometric design, drainage design, safety documentation, standards interpretation, or integrated written justification. This indicates that AI in road design is presently more mature as a decision-support and optimisation tool than as a fully autonomous design system.

Broader engineering studies help clarify what more advanced automation might look like, even when they are not directly about roads. Daareyni et al. (2025), for example, show that large language model-based systems can translate text and visual inputs into CAD outputs and refine those outputs through iterative feedback loops. This is not direct evidence of AI road design, but it is a useful analogy for the long-term objective of converting basic project information into design outputs through a connected workflow. Taken together, these sources suggest that AI applications in road design currently operate most convincingly in optimisation and decision support, while broader engineering research points toward a future in which AI may connect input requirements, technical reasoning, model generation, and revision in a more end-to-end design process.
